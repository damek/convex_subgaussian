Definition 2Explicit constants

The explicit constant \(c_0\) is built from the sub-Gaussian tail cap

\[ s_G(t) := \min \{ 1, 2e^{-t^2/2}\} , \qquad t_0 := \sqrt{2 \log 2}, \]

the total tail mass

\[ A := \int _0^\infty s_G(t)\, dt, \qquad B := A/2, \]

the unique threshold \(a {\gt} t_0\) solving

\[ H(a) = a s_G(a) + \int _a^\infty s_G(t)\, dt = B, \]

the height \(p_0 := s_G(a)\), the Gaussian tail quantile \(z\) satisfying \(\overline{\Phi }(z) = p_0\), and finally

\[ c_0 := \frac{B}{\varphi (z)}. \]

Definition 1Admissible scale and sharp constant

A positive number \(c\) is admissible if every centered real random variable \(X\) with the two-sided sub-Gaussian tail bound above satisfies

\[ \mathbb {E}[f(X)] \le \mathbb {E}[f(cG)] \]

for every convex function \(f\) whose expectations on both sides are finite. The sharp constant \(c_\star \) is the infimum of all admissible scales.

Lemma 9The threshold parameter \(a\) and the tangent line

There is a unique \(a {\gt} t_0\) such that \(H(a)=B\), and the resulting envelope \(J_G\) lies above the affine function \(u \mapsto B - p_0 u\) on \([0,\infty )\).

Lemma 12Closed form for the Gaussian stop-loss transform

If \(G \sim \mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(c{\gt}0\), then the stop-loss transform of \(cG\) is

\[ g_c(u) = c\, \varphi (u/c) - u\, \overline{\Phi }(u/c). \]

Lemma 13Tangent-line contact at \(u_\star = c_0 z\)

At the point \(u_\star := c_0 z\), the Gaussian stop-loss at scale \(c_0\) has slope \(-p_0\) and value \(B - p_0 u_\star \). By convexity of the Gaussian stop-loss,

\[ g_{c_0}(u) \ge B - p_0 u \qquad \text{for all } u \in \mathbb {R}. \]

Lemma 14Monotone-ratio argument

\[ R(u) := \frac{\overline{\Phi }(u/c_0)}{2e^{-u^2/2}} \]

is increasing on \([a,\infty )\), and the corresponding monotone-ratio principle converts this monotonicity into a comparison between the Gaussian stop-loss and the tail-integral branch of the envelope.

Theorem 8Convex domination from stop-loss comparison

If the Gaussian stop-loss transform dominates the envelope \(J_G\) pointwise, then every centered sub-Gaussian law is convex-dominated by the Gaussian comparator at that same scale.

Theorem 6Sharp stop-loss envelope under the sub-Gaussian cap

Assume that \(\mu \) is centered and satisfies the tail cap \(\mathbb {P}_\mu (|x|{\gt}t)\le s_G(t)\) for every \(t \ge 0\). Then for every \(u \ge 0\),

\[ \mathrm{stopLoss}_\mu (u) \le J_G(u), \]

where \(J_G\) is the explicit envelope built from the constants \(B\), \(a\), and \(p_0\).

Theorem 7Extension to all thresholds

Theorem 10Extremal measure saturating the tail cap

There exists an explicit probability measure \(\mu ^\star \) with mean zero such that

\[ \mathbb {P}_{\mu ^\star }(|x|{\gt}t) = s_G(t) \qquad (t \ge 0). \]

This is the canonical witness showing that the stop-loss envelope is sharp.

Theorem 16Sharp one-dimensional convex sub-Gaussian comparison

The sharp admissible Gaussian scale is \(c_\star = c_0\). Equivalently:

  1. Every centered real random variable satisfying \(\mathbb {P}(|X|{\gt}t)\le 2e^{-t^2/2}\) for all \(t \ge 0\) is convex-dominated by the centered Gaussian law of scale \(c_0\).

  2. For every \(c {\lt} c_0\), convex domination by the Gaussian law of scale \(c\) fails, already for the canonical witness \(\mu ^\star \) and an explicit hinge test function.

Theorem 3Domination and sharpness

  1. Domination. Every centered sub-Gaussian random variable is convex-dominated by the Gaussian law at scale \(c_0\).

  2. Sharpness. For every \(c {\lt} c_0\), domination fails: the canonical witness measure \(\mu ^\star \) and a hinge test function \(x \mapsto (x-u)_+\) exhibit a strict inequality in the opposite direction.
